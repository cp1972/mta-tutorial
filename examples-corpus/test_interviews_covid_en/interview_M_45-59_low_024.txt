Q: What lessons do you take from this period?
R: At my age, the prospect of losing my job and finding another was scary. My parents were elderly and I lived in fear of bringing the virus home. Every time someone in the family coughed, the anxiety came back. I worried that age discrimination would make a job search impossible.

Q: What role did digital tools play in your daily life?
R: Setting up the home office, the VPN, the dual monitors took some work. I had to learn to lead meetings on video, it changed my management style.

Q: What changed the most in your daily life during the pandemic?
R: I had to learn many new digital tools faster than I would have liked. Some relationships deepened, others quietly faded during the long months. I had to ask my family for help with the bills, which I had never done.

Q: Did you worry about your health or the health of people close to you?
R: I went to the food bank for the first time in my life, it was humbling. Video calls replaced almost all face-to-face interaction for months.

Q: How did you experience the lockdown periods?
R: Society proved more fragile than I thought but also more resilient. I redesigned my workflow around asynchronous communication tools.

Q: How did you feel about masks and physical distancing rules?
R: Childcare and household tasks combined left no time for myself. Booster doses became part of the routine, like an annual habit. I had to learn many new digital tools faster than I would have liked. The shift to remote work was difficult at my age, the technology was new.

Q: How do you look back on the vaccination campaign?
R: Productivity actually improved without commuting and office distractions. I rediscovered email and phone calls after years of mostly messaging.
