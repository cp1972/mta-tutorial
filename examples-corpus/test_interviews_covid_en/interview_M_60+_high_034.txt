Q: What changed the most in your daily life during the pandemic?
R: Society proved more fragile than I thought but also more resilient. The isolation was harder than the fear of the illness itself.

Q: How do you look back on the vaccination campaign?
R: Some rules seemed inconsistent and that undermined public trust. The mortgage was a worry but we kept up with payments. I was advised to stay home for months because of my age and health. I used the savings to renovate our house and improve our home office.

Q: What lessons do you take from this period?
R: I felt forgotten by the world during those long quiet months. I had to learn to lead meetings on video, it changed my management style. Booster doses became part of the routine, like an annual habit. We supported local businesses since we had the means to do so.

Q: Did you find the restrictions justified or excessive?
R: We supported local businesses since we had the means to do so. I started paying much more attention to my general health and immunity. I had complicated feelings about masking children in schools.

Q: How did your social relationships evolve during the pandemic?
R: I redesigned my workflow around asynchronous communication tools. I was advised to stay home for months because of my age and health. I went weeks without seeing anyone in person, only voices on the phone. The mental health consequences are still with us, I think, even now.

Q: How did you handle work or income during this period?
R: Video calls replaced almost all face-to-face interaction for months. I sometimes still wear a mask in crowded places out of habit. The mental health consequences are still with us, I think, even now.

Q: How did you experience the lockdown periods?
R: At my age, the prospect of losing my job and finding another was scary. Keeping physical distance from people felt strange and unnatural at first. I redesigned my workflow around asynchronous communication tools. Streaming services kept us entertained through the long evenings indoors.
