Q: Did you worry about your health or the health of people close to you?
R: I had to ask my family for help with the bills, which I had never done. Helping with homework on top of my own work was completely draining.

Q: How did you experience the lockdown periods?
R: Productivity actually improved without commuting and office distractions. The kids missed their friends terribly and it broke my heart to see. The mental health consequences are still with us, I think, even now. Job interviews on video felt strange, I struggled to make a connection.

Q: How do you look back on the vaccination campaign?
R: The eviction moratorium probably saved us from being homeless. Some rules seemed inconsistent and that undermined public trust. The mask debates in the media were exhausting to follow.

Q: How did you feel about masks and physical distancing rules?
R: Productivity actually improved without commuting and office distractions. Looking back, the response could have been faster and more coordinated. I joined virtual social events, which felt strange but better than nothing. I had a sore arm and tiredness for a day, but nothing worse than that.

Q: What changed the most in your daily life during the pandemic?
R: The kids missed their friends terribly and it broke my heart to see. Knowing someone in intensive care really brought the threat home.

Q: How did your social relationships evolve during the pandemic?
R: Outdoor walks with one friend at a time became precious moments. Some relationships deepened, others quietly faded during the long months.

Q: What role did digital tools play in your daily life?
R: I trusted the public health authorities even when their advice changed. The emotional support for the whole family became my unspoken job. I had to learn to lead meetings on video, it changed my management style.
