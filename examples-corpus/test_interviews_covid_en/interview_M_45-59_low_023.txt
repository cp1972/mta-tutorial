Q: How did you experience the lockdown periods?
R: I noticed I was the one keeping track of supplies, medicines, schedules. The mortgage was a worry but we kept up with payments. I postponed dental care and other things because money was scarce. The eviction moratorium probably saved us from being homeless.

Q: What changed the most in your daily life during the pandemic?
R: Productivity actually improved without commuting and office distractions. I postponed dental care and other things because money was scarce. I think the pandemic forced us to reconsider what really matters in life.

Q: How did your social relationships evolve during the pandemic?
R: I used some of our savings to get through the uncertain months. I joined virtual social events, which felt strange but better than nothing.

Q: Did you worry about your health or the health of people close to you?
R: At my age, the prospect of losing my job and finding another was scary. Coordinating projects across distributed teams was a real shift. I postponed dental care and other things because money was scarce.

Q: What role did digital tools play in your daily life?
R: Reading the daily case numbers became an unhealthy obsession for a while. Productivity actually improved without commuting and office distractions. Money was very tight during those months, I had to cut everything. The emotional support for the whole family became my unspoken job.

Q: How do you look back on the vaccination campaign?
R: Reconnecting with people after restrictions lifted was sometimes awkward. Family video calls became the highlight of my week.

Q: What lessons do you take from this period?
R: The vaccination process was well organized in my area, very efficient. Money was very tight during those months, I had to cut everything. Some things changed permanently, like how we view work and where we live. I rediscovered email and phone calls after years of mostly messaging.
