Q: How do you look back on the vaccination campaign?
R: I rediscovered email and phone calls after years of mostly messaging. Reconnecting with people after restrictions lifted was sometimes awkward. I joined virtual social events, which felt strange but better than nothing. Productivity actually improved without commuting and office distractions.

Q: Did you worry about your health or the health of people close to you?
R: I noticed I was the one keeping track of supplies, medicines, schedules. I postponed dental care and other things because money was scarce.

Q: What changed the most in your daily life during the pandemic?
R: Unemployment benefits arrived late and were not enough to live on. I had a sore arm and tiredness for a day, but nothing worse than that. Knowing someone in intensive care really brought the threat home.

Q: How did your social relationships evolve during the pandemic?
R: I joined virtual social events, which felt strange but better than nothing. Booster doses became part of the routine, like an annual habit.

Q: What role did digital tools play in your daily life?
R: Childcare and household tasks combined left no time for myself. I moved back in with my parents because I could not afford rent alone. I learned who my real friends were when we could not meet in person.

Q: What lessons do you take from this period?
R: Some relationships deepened, others quietly faded during the long months. I rediscovered email and phone calls after years of mostly messaging. I had a sore arm and tiredness for a day, but nothing worse than that.

Q: How did you experience the lockdown periods?
R: Unemployment benefits arrived late and were not enough to live on. Paying rent became a real source of anxiety, every month was a battle. Job interviews on video felt strange, I struggled to make a connection.
